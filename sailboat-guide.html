How I Got on a Sailboat
for 40 Days

I looked everywhere for a clear answer to this question before I left. I couldn't find it in one place. This is everything I know — the programs, the agencies, the costs, and what I wish someone had told me.

✓ Completely free — no email required
Path 01 — Structured Programs
Seamester & Academic Voyages

This is the path I took. Seamester runs semester-at-sea programs aboard their tall ships — you earn real college credits (USF accredited), get SCUBA certified, and sail routes like Palau to Bali with a crew of 30. It's not cheap, but it's structured, safe, and gives you credentials that open doors afterward.

Seamester
Academic
Full semester programs in the Pacific, Caribbean, and Mediterranean. 3 USF college credits, dive certifications included, safety staff onboard. Apply 6–9 months out. My program ran Palau → Raja Ampat → Komodo → Bali.
💲 $15,000–$22,000 📅 Apply 6–9 months ahead 🌐 seamester.com
Semester at Sea
Academic
Larger ship (700+ students), more ports, University of Virginia accredited. Less intimate than Seamester but more destinations per voyage. Good if you want to collect ports rather than dive deep into a region.
💲 $25,000–$30,000 📅 Apply 9–12 months ahead 🌐 semesteratsea.org
Path 02 — Crew Agencies
Find-a-Crew & Work Exchange

If you want to get on a boat without paying program fees, crew agencies are how most people do it. You list your availability and skills; boat owners post open positions. Some are paid, some are food-and-bunk exchanges, some ask for a small contribution to expenses. The more flexible your schedule, the more options open up.

Find-a-Crew
Crew platform
The largest crew placement platform. Boats post open positions; you apply. Works best for experienced sailors but there are beginner-friendly passages, especially in the Caribbean and Pacific.
💲 Free to browse, ~$50/year for full access 🌐 find-a-crew.net
Crewseekers
Crew platform
UK-based but global listings. Strong Atlantic and Mediterranean options. Interface is older but the listings are genuine. Good for finding longer offshore passages.
💲 ~£40/year 🌐 crewseekers.net
Yacht delivery work
Get paid
Owners need boats moved between locations — marinas, across oceans, after purchases. Delivery companies hire crew for legs. You can get paid while logging offshore miles. Requires some experience but not as much as you'd think for shorter legs.
💲 Paid (~$100–200/day) 📅 Flexible, short-notice often
Honest advice
What I wish I'd known before I left
The things that actually matter
  • Budget 30–50% more than the listed program cost — flights, gear, visas, and incidentals add up fast
  • Get your Open Water certification before you go — every program gives you more time in the water if you're not starting from zero
  • A dry bag, a good headlamp, and seasickness medication are more important than any piece of tech you bring
  • For crew agency positions, your first boat won't be your best one — take it anyway and use it to get references for the next
  • The social dynamic of a small boat is the thing nobody warns you about — 8 people in 40 feet is intense, and that's fine, but know it going in
The stuff nobody mentions
Unexpected but Valuable Expenses

Everyone budgets for the big ticket items. These are the things that didn't make the original list but turned out to matter — either because they made the trip significantly better, or because I wished I'd had them from day one.

Dive Computer
Safety
The rental ones work but you'll want your own once you're doing multiple dives a day. A personal dive computer tracks your nitrogen loading across dives, not just the current one. Entry-level models from Garmin or Suunto start around $250 and are worth every cent if you're doing 10+ dives.
💲 $250–$600🤿 Buy before you go
Travel Insurance
Safety
Not optional for a trip like this. Medical evacuation from a remote Indonesian island can cost $50,000+. World Nomads specifically covers scuba diving and sailing — most standard travel insurance policies exclude both. Get a policy that covers emergency evacuation, trip interruption, and dive accidents.
💲 $150–$400 for 40 days🌐 worldnomads.com
eSIM
Connectivity
A physical SIM swap in Palau, Indonesia, and Bali is a bureaucratic headache. An eSIM from Airalo or Holafly lets you buy a local data plan digitally before you land. Invaluable for port days when you need to post content, check weather, or communicate with people at home.
💲 $15–$40 per country🌐 airalo.com
Knot Tying Book
Skill
Sounds old-fashioned. It isn't. Knowing 8–10 essential sailing knots makes you immediately useful on deck and earns respect from experienced crew. The Marlinspike Sailor or Ashley Book of Knots are the classics. Study it before you leave — the boat is not the place to learn from scratch.
💲 $15–$25📚 Learn before departure
Action Camera
Content
A GoPro or DJI Osmo Action is your underwater and deck camera. Your phone won't survive the salt spray and can't go to depth. Get a proper underwater housing rated to at least 40m if you're diving seriously. Buy extra batteries — you'll burn through them faster than you think, and there's no Amazon delivery at sea.
💲 $300–$500 with housing📷 Buy + test before departure
Hammock
Quality of life
A lightweight nylon hammock (ENO makes the best) weighs almost nothing and completely transforms port days. Every island stop with trees becomes a rest spot. Also useful on deck during calm downwind passages. One of the highest enjoyment-per-gram items you can bring.
💲 $50–$80⚖️ Under 500g packed
Strong Carabiners
Safety
Not the decorative kind — proper locking carabiners rated to at least 20kN. Use them to clip your gear bag to a jackline, secure your camera to your wrist in big swell, or clip into safety lines on rough passages. Bring 4–6. They cost $10–$15 each and are one of those items where you really don't want the cheap version.
💲 $10–$15 each🔒 Locking gate only
